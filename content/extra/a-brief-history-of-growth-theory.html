A Brief History of Modern Growth Theory

Notes taken from Barro's Economic Growth

Chen Zhou

Sep 13 2016

Classical Economists

  • Adam Smith (1776)
  • David Ricardo (1817)
  • Thomas Malthus (1798)
and much latter
  • Frank Ramsey (1928)
  • Allyn Young (1928)
  • Frank Knight (1944)
  • Joseph Schumpeter (1934)

Those who brought us building blocks

  • Basic approaches of competitive behavior and equilibrium dynamics
  • The role of diminishing returns
  • Growth by accumulation of physical and human capital
  • The interplay between per capita income and the growth rate of population
  • Technological pregress by labor specialization and discovering new goods and methods of production
  • The role of monopoly power in technological advance

Modern Growth Theory

Starting Point

Ramsey, a man beyond his time

  • Househould optimization overtime

  • Intertemporally separable utility function

Before the Late 1950s

Harrod (1939) and Domar (1946)
  • Integrate Keynesian Analysis
  • Production functions with little substitutability among the inputs
  • Capitalist system is inherently unstable

Play little role in today' thinking

1950s

STart of The Neoclassical Growth Model

Solow (1956) and Swan (1956)
  • The neoclassical form of the production function
    • Constant returns to scale
    • Diminishing returns to inputs
    • Prositive and smooth elasticity of substitution
  • Conditional convergence
    • The lower the starting level of per capita GDP, the faster the growth rate
    • The steady-state levels of capital and output per workd depend on variable characteristics
  • In the absence of continuing improvements in technology, per capita growth must eventually cease.
  • Technological progress occured exogenously
1960s
The Basic Neoclassical Growth Model
Cass (1965) and Koopmans (1965)
  • Ramsey's analysis of consumer optimation
  • Does not eliminate theexogenous technological progress
  • The decentralized outcome are Pareto optimal
Attempt to include technological change

Arrow (1962) and Shesinski (1967)

  • Learn by doing
  • The resulting growth rate would not be Pareto optimal
Till the early 1970s
Declining
  • Excessively technical
  • Steadily lost contact with empirical applications
  • Focus on short-term fluctuations
After the mid-1980s

Boom: Endogenous growth model

The long-term per capita growth rate was not pegged by the rate of exogenous technological pregress any longer.

Romer (1986), Lucas (1988), Rebelo (1991)

  • Returns to capital (human capital) do not necessarily diminish
  • Growth may go on indefinately

Romer (1987, 1990), Aghion and Howitt (1992) and Grossman and Helpman (1991)

  • Incorporation of R&D theories
  • and imperfect competition
  • The rate of growth tends not to be Pareto optimal
  • Long-term growth rate depends on governmental actions
New research
  • Scale effects in the growth process (Jones, 1999)
  • Technological progress to be labor or capital augmenting (Acemoglu, 2002)
  • The role of competion in the growth process (Aghion et al., 2001, 2002)
  • Diffusion of technology by imitation
  • Endogenous population growth by incorporating an anaylysis of fertility choice into the neoclassical model
