The Neoclassical Production Function

Constant returns to scale

$$F(\lambda K, \lambda L, T) = \lambda \cdot F(K, L, T) \text{ for all } \lambda > 0$$

Positive and diminishing returns to inputs

\begin{align} \frac{\partial F}{\partial K} > 0, & \quad \frac{\partial^2F}{\partial K^2} < 0 \\ \frac{\partial F}{\partial L} > 0, & \quad \frac{\partial^2 F}{\partial L^2} < 0. \end{align}

Inada consitions

\begin{gather} \lim_{K \to 0} \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial K} \right) = \lim_{L \to 0} \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial L} \right) = \infty \\ \lim_{K \to \infty} \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial K} \right) = \lim_{L \to \infty} \left( \frac{\partial F}{\partial L} \right) = 0 \end{gather}

Essentiality

$$F(0,L) = F(K, 0) = 0$$

The fundamental equation of the Solow-Swan Model

The net increase in the stock of physical capital equals gross investment less depreciation.

  • Saving rate: \(s\)
  • Output: \(Y = F(K,L,T)\)
  • Investment: \(I = sY\)
  • Depreciation: \(\delta K\)
  • \(\dot{K} = I - \delta K\)

Transform into a differential equation in terms of \(k\)

$$k \equiv K/L$$

$$\dot{k} = \frac{d(K/L)}{dt} = \dot{K}/L - nk$$

$$\dot{K}/L = s \cdot f(k) - \delta k$$

The fundamental differential equation

$$\dot{k} = s \cdot f(k) - (n+\delta)\cdot k$$

Markets

The fundamental equation of the Solow-Swan model can be derived in a framework that explicitly incorporates markets

Firms rent capital from the households

  • The rental price for capital: \(R\)
  • Capital stocks depreciation rate: \(\delta \ge 0\)
  • The net return of capital to households: \(R - \delta\)
  • Interest rate \(r = R - \delta\)
  • \(R = r + \delta\)

Firm's choice on the ratio of capital to labor

\begin{align} \max_{k} \pi & = F(K, L, T) - (r + \delta) \cdot K - wL \\ & = L \cdot [f(k) - (r + \delta) \cdot k - w] \end{align}

Therefore

$$ f'(k) = r + \delta $$

Interest rate

$$ r = f'(k) - \delta$$

Wage rate equals the marginal product of labor

\begin{gather} Y = L \cdot f(K/L) \\ w = \partial Y / \partial L = f(k) - k \cdot f'(k) \end{gather}

Assets per capita \(a\)

$$a = \text{assets} / L$$

\begin{align} \dot{a} & = (\dot{\text{assets}} - \text{assets}\cdot \dot{L}) / L^2 \\ & = (1/L) \cdot \dot{\text{assets}} - na \end{align}

$$\dot{\text{assets}} = [r\cdot (\text{assets}) + w\cdot L] - C$$

$$ \dot{a} = (r\cdot a + w) - c - na $$

Equilibrium: assets per capita \(a\) = capital per capita \(k\)

$$ \dot{k} = f(k) - c - (n + \delta) \cdot k $$

$$c = (1-s) \cdot f(k) $$

$$\dot{k} = s\cdot f(k) - (n+\delta)k$$

The Steady State

$$\dot{k}=0$$

$$s\cdot f(k^*) = (n+\delta)\cdot k^*$$

\(k\) is constant in the steady state

so is \(y^* = f(k^*)\) and \(c^* = (1-s)\cdot f(k^*)\)

The levels of variables, \(K, Y\) and \(C\), grow at the rate of population growth \(n\)

Golden Rule

Do unto others as you would have others do unto you.

The steady-state level of per capita consumption

\begin{align} c^* & = (1-s) \cdot f[k^*(s)] \\ & = f[k^*(s)] - (n+\delta) \cdot k^*(s) \end{align}

\(s \cdot f(k^*) = (n+\delta) \cdot k^*\)

Maximize \(c^*\) by \(k_{\text{gold}}\)

$$ f'(k_{\text{gold}}) = n + \delta $$

Transitional Dynamics

The growth rate of \(k\)

$$\gamma_k = \dot{k}/k = s \cdot f(k) / k - (n + \delta)$$

The behavior of output along the transition

$$\dot{y}/y = f'(k) \cdot \dot{k} / f(k) = [k \cdot f'(k) / f(k)] \cdot (\dot{k}/k)$$

The relation between \(\dot{y}/y\) and \(\dot{k}/k\) depends on the behavior of the capital share

$$ \dot{y}/y = s \cdot f'(k) - (n + \delta) \cdot \mathrm{Sh}(k) $$ where \(\mathrm{Sh}(k) \equiv k\cdot f'(k) / f(k)\)

\begin{multline} \partial (\dot{y}/y) / \partial k = \left[ \frac{f''(k) \cdot k}{f(k)} \right] \cdot (\dot{k}/k) - \\ \frac{(n+\delta)f'(k)}{f(k)} \cdot [1 - \mathrm{Sh}(k)] \end{multline}

Consumption exhibits the same dynmics as output

$$c = ( 1- s) \cdot y$$ $$\dot{c}/c = \dot{y}/y$$

Behavior of Input Prices During the Transition

Interest rate declines monotonically toward its steady-state value

$$r = f'(k) - \delta$$ $$\partial r / \partial k = f''(k) < 0$$

Wage rate increases monotonically as the capital stock grows

\begin{gather} w = f(k) - k \cdot f'(k) \\ \frac{\partial w}{\partial k} = f'(k) - f'(k) - k \cdot f''(k) = - k \cdot f''(k) > 0 \end{gather}

Policy Experiments

The economy adjusts according to the saving rate

  • a permanent increase in the saving rate generates temporarily positive per capita growth rates
  • the levels of \(k\) and \(y\) are permanently higher
  • but the per capita growth rates return to zero

The growth rate of population

  • A decrease in \(n\) shifts the depreciation line downward
  • The steady-state level of capital per worker would be larger
  • The long-run growth rate of capital per person would remain at zero

Technology

  • The production function \(f(k)\) shifts upward, the saving curve shfits upward
  • In the long run, the permanent improvement generates higher levels of \(k\) and \(y\)
  • But no changes in the per capita growth rates
  • Improvements in knowledge are not bounded
  • Growth must come from technological progress
